10.1 Email

A technique of sending and receiving messages and files via the internet — used in personal, business and marketing communications.

Key Email Concepts

  • Cc (Carbon Copy) — send email to multiple recipients; all recipients can see each other
  • Bcc (Blind Carbon Copy) — send to multiple recipients without them knowing it was sent to others
  • Forward — send an existing email to a new recipient
  • Attachments — files attached and sent with an email
  • Email Groups — enable mass communication to a specific group of subscribers

Netiquette

Proper conduct and politeness when using email:

  • Don't be abusive; don't send spam
  • Be clear and concise
  • Remember posts are usually public
  • Always check spelling and grammar
  • Respect people's privacy; don't embarrass others
  • Forgive people's mistakes
  • Do NOT use ALL CAPITALS to highlight comments
  • Do not plagiarise
  • Do not use too many emoticons

Email Guidelines & Spam

Employer Guidelines for Email Use

  • Company decides if personal emails are permitted on their systems
  • Specifies which devices may be used for company email
  • Standard style required for company emails
  • Clear rules on what email content is not permitted
  • Employees must only use their own accounts
  • Clear rules on confidentiality of information
  • Staff informed of how long emails are stored
  • Incoming emails read only by intended recipient
  • Company policy on dealing with/preventing viruses
  • Company has the right to monitor and read all emails — staff must be aware
  • Ongoing training to ensure compliance with company policy

Spam

Any unwanted email delivered via the internet — can range from annoying to genuinely harmful (may contain viruses).

Disadvantages:

  • Wastes time
  • Annoys users
  • Uses up bandwidth, slowing the internet
  • May contain viruses or phishing attempts
  • Takes up significant inbox space

To Minimise Spam:

  • Use spam filters
  • Avoid disclosing your email address publicly
  • Don't click links in spam emails or reply to them

10.2 The Internet

Characteristics of the Internet

  • Allows people worldwide to connect and chat instantaneously
  • Runs on a distributed network — no single central authority controls it
  • Transmits data in many formats: text, photos, audio, video

Advantages of the Internet:

  • Access to enormous amounts of information — empowers learning
  • Connects individuals and businesses globally in real-time
  • Makes many tasks easier and more effective

Disadvantages of the Internet:

  • Security and privacy risks — identity theft, data breaches, online monitoring
  • Spread of misinformation and fake news
  • Digital divide — unequal internet access widens existing disparities

Internet vs World Wide Web

These terms are NOT interchangeable:

  • Internet — the global network of computers and devices connected through routers and servers
  • World Wide Web (WWW) — a collection of websites and webpages accessed via the internet

Uses of the Internet:

  • Communication — email, instant messaging
  • Information database — almost any subject
  • Commerce — online shopping, electronic payments, global business

Blogs, Forums, Wikis & Social Networking

Blog

Website sharing information informally (like a journal). Reverse chronological order. Usually by individuals or small groups. Allows reader comments. Focuses on specific topics.

Forum

Online discussion site with conversations in posted messages. Organised into threads by topic. Peer-to-peer interaction. May require account to post.

A moderated forum has an administrator who checks posts before they are published. The internet itself is essentially an unmoderated forum — no single owner.

Wiki

Website allowing users to add, remove or edit content. Facilitates collaboration and knowledge sharing. Not in chronological order. Changes can be tracked and reverted. Most famous example: Wikipedia.

Social Networking

Platforms connecting users to share content (Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, LinkedIn). Users create profiles; share text, images, videos. Privacy settings control who sees content.

Internet Functionality — ISP, URL & Hyperlinks

ISP — Internet Service Provider

A company that provides internet access, typically charging a monthly fee. Creates user accounts with login credentials (user ID and password).

URL — Uniform Resource Locator

The address of a web page on the WWW. Uniquely identifies the location of any internet resource.

Three components:

  • Protocol — communication rules (HTTP, HTTPS, FTP)
  • Domain — name of the server (or IP address)
  • Webpage/filename — location of the file on the server
Example: https://www.hoddereducation.co.uk/IGCSE/ICT
Protocol: https · Domain: www.hoddereducation.co.uk · Path: IGCSE · File: ICT

Hyperlink & Web Browser

Hyperlink — a word, phrase or image referencing data the reader can follow by clicking, usually taking them to another web page.

Web Browser — software application used to locate, retrieve and display content on the WWW, including web pages, images, video and other files.

Finding Information

  • Method 1: Enter the URL directly if you know the address
  • Method 2: Use a search engine to locate information
  • Narrow searches using: quotes for phrases, + for required terms, - for excluded terms

Using Search Engines

Advantages of Using the Internet for Information:

  • Tends to be up to date — easier and quicker to amend
  • Vast amounts of information available
  • Searching is fast and easy
  • Can be done from the comfort of home
  • Information is essentially free of charge
  • Webpages may have multimedia elements making learning more interesting

Disadvantages:

  • Not regulated — anything can be posted
  • Risk of accessing inappropriate websites
  • Too easy to be distracted
  • Some research skills are lost as search engines do the work

Evaluating Information Found Online

  • Consider the source's reputation and trustworthiness
  • Check accuracy by comparing with other reliable sources
  • Assess whether information is biased — pushing a particular viewpoint?
  • Check if the material is current — outdated information can be misleading
  • Reliable sources: educational (.edu), government (.gov), well-known industry websites

Risks of Using the Internet

  • Inappropriate and unlawful content available
  • Data restriction controls: parental, educational, ISP filters

Internet Protocols

Protocols — rules on which the sender and recipient agree when data is exchanged between devices.

ProtocolFull NamePurpose
HTTP HyperText Transfer Protocol Used when accessing web pages. Tells the browser to follow HTTP communication rules.
HTTPS HyperText Transfer Protocol Secure Secure version of HTTP — encrypts data for security. The 'S' indicates a secure, encrypted connection.
FTP File Transfer Protocol Network protocol specifically used when transferring files from one computer to another over the internet.
SSL Secure Sockets Layer Protocol allowing data to be sent and received securely over the internet. Creates encrypted links between client and server.
