8.1 Physical Safety Hazards

HazardCausesPrevention Strategies
Electrocution Spilling liquids near electrical equipment; touching live cables; open/exposed cables Keep liquids away from equipment; ensure cables are fully insulated; use circuit breakers/fuses to prevent overload
Fire Socket overload (too many devices in one socket); overheating equipment; blocked ventilation Ensure enough sockets; don't overload sockets; keep equipment ventilated; don't cover air vents; keep flammable materials away; turn off/unplug devices when leaving; use fire extinguishers
Trailing Cables Cables protruding across walking areas — can cause trips, falls, broken bones, sprains Use cable ties to secure cables; keep cables in cable channels; use wireless devices where possible; regularly inspect cables for wear/damage
Heavy Falling Equipment Equipment placed near desk edges; unstable surfaces Place equipment on solid, stable surfaces away from edges; regularly check stability; inspect equipment and locations for signs of wear/damage

8.2 eSafety — Data Protection

The Data Protection Act (DPA) and GDPR control personal data collection, storage and processing.

Principles of the Data Protection Act

  • Data must be processed lawfully, fairly and transparently with clear consent
  • Data collected only for specific, explicit, legitimate purposes
  • Only the minimum necessary data should be collected and retained
  • Data must be accurate and up-to-date
  • Data should not be kept longer than necessary
  • Data must be protected against unauthorised processing, loss or damage

Why is Data Protection Legislation Required?

  • Protects individuals' right to privacy and control over personal information
  • Prevents identity theft, fraud and misuse of personal data
  • Builds trust between individuals and organisations
  • Promotes responsible data handling practices
  • Grants individuals rights: access, rectification, erasure, objection

Personal Data & Protection

Personal Data

Information that can be used to identify an individual:

  • Personal name
  • Address
  • Date of birth
  • Photograph in school uniform
  • Medical history

Sensitive Data

Data that could cause loss of advantage/security if disclosed: ethnicity, religion, criminal record.

Threats from Unprotected Data

  • Identity theft
  • Privacy breaches
  • Misuse of information
  • Data sold to third parties
  • Individuals held to ransom
  • Used to commit physical crimes

How to Avoid Inappropriate Data Disclosure

  • Keep data confidential using privacy settings and strong passwords
  • Limit access to authorised individuals only
  • Think before you post — consider what info can be gathered from content
  • Check website details about data collection, storage and use
  • Only access personal data sites on secure, encrypted connections

eSafety

eSafety — knowing how to use the internet safely and responsibly; protecting oneself from potential dangers online.

Internet Safety

  • Use trusted websites recommended by teachers
  • Use age-appropriate search engines with filters
  • Never reveal personal information

Email Safety

  • Be aware of dangers of opening emails from unknown people (phishing, viruses)
  • Only send personal data/images to people you know
  • Avoid sending identifiable content (e.g. school photos)

Social Media Safety

  • Know how to block and report unwanted users
  • Use privacy settings to control who sees your posts
  • Never meet online contacts face-to-face alone; if you do, take an adult and meet publicly
  • Do not distribute inappropriate images or language
  • Only accept friend requests from people you know
  • Do not post images/details revealing your location

Online Gaming Safety

Do not use real names as usernames · Never share personal or financial details · Know how to block and report players for inappropriate messages

8.3 Security Threats to Data

ThreatDescriptionPrevention
HackingUnauthorised access to systems to steal info or cause damage → identity theft, privacy breachesStrong passwords; 2FA; keep software updated; firewalls; antivirus
PhishingDeceptive emails mimicking trusted sources to trick users into revealing personal data — user must take actionDon't click suspicious links; check sender; look for spelling errors; use spam filters; only enter data on HTTPS sites
PharmingMalicious DNS manipulation redirecting users to fake websites — no action needed from userType addresses manually; keep antivirus updated; use DNS protection tools
SmishingPhishing carried out through SMS text messagesDon't click unknown text links; verify with official sources; block/report suspicious numbers
VishingVoice phishing — tricks users into calling a malicious number via voicemailDon't give personal info over phone; hang up and call official numbers directly; beware of urgent/threatening calls
Viruses & MalwareSelf-replicating code that corrupts/deletes data; malicious software disrupting systemsInstall/update antivirus; avoid downloading from unknown sources; don't open suspicious attachments; keep OS updated
Card FraudUnauthorised use of card info via shoulder surfing, card cloning, keyloggingDon't share card details; use secure payment sites; check bank statements regularly; enable transaction alerts
Trojan HorseMalicious software disguised as legitimate softwareDownload only from trusted sources; don't open unknown attachments; use antivirus
WormsStandalone malware that spreads through networks automaticallyKeep systems updated with security patches; use firewalls; avoid opening unsafe files

Methods of Protecting Data

  • Biometrics — uses unique physical/behavioural traits (fingerprints, face, iris). Hard to fake or duplicate. Used in phone unlock (Face ID), airport security, banking. Note: biometric data cannot be reset if stolen.
  • Digital Certificate — digital record attesting to a website's reliability. Contains identifier data, entity's public key, and third-party digital signature. Builds trust between parties.
  • SSL (Secure Socket Layer) — protocol creating a secure connection between client and server. Ensures data stays private and cannot be intercepted. Identified by the S in HTTPS.
  • Encryption — converts data to a format that cannot be read without a decryption key. Protects hard drives, emails, cloud storage, HTTPS websites. Even if data is stolen, it cannot be decoded without the key.
  • Firewall — network security device that monitors and manages incoming/outgoing traffic. Separates internal network from external networks by filtering data. Prevents malware, unauthorised access and other network risks.
  • Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) — requires two different forms of identification: something you know (password) + something you have (smartphone/token) or are (biometrics). Provides extra protection beyond username and password.
  • User ID and Password — typical authentication using a secret code + password. Restricts access to authorised users only. Must use a mix of uppercase, lowercase, digits and special characters. Must be changed regularly.

Topic 8 — Key Points

8.1 Physical Safety

  • Electrocution — keep liquids away; insulate cables; use fuses
  • Fire — don't overload sockets; ensure ventilation; unplug when away
  • Trailing Cables — cable ties; wireless devices; cable channels
  • Falling Equipment — secure placement; stable surfaces; away from edges

8.2 eSafety

  • DPA/GDPR: lawful, minimal, accurate, secure data handling

8.3 Threats & Protections

  • Threats: Hacking · Phishing · Pharming · Smishing · Vishing · Viruses · Card fraud · Trojans · Worms
  • Protections:
  • Biometrics — physical traits, hard to fake
  • Digital Certificate — website trust verification
  • SSL — encrypted client-server connection
  • Encryption — unreadable without decryption key
  • Firewall — filters incoming/outgoing traffic
  • 2FA — two types of identification required
  • User ID + Password — with mix of characters; changed regularly
